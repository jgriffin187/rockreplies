How to tell if it's a real agate

▾
Banding Look for parallel or concentric bands that curve and wrap around each other like tree rings — straight, wavy, or eyed patterns are all common. This is the single most reliable sign of an agate.
Color palette Lake Superior agates are famous for red, orange, and rust bands (from iron oxide) alternating with cream, white, or gray bands. Solid gray or all-white chalcedony without banding is usually not a "keeper" agate.
Luster & surface Agate is a form of chalcedony (quartz), so it has a smooth, waxy-to-glassy luster and breaks with a smooth, curved (conchoidal) fracture — never a flat, dull, or crumbly surface like basalt or sandstone.
Translucency Hold the rock up to sunlight or a flashlight. Real agate lets some light glow through at the thinner edges; opaque, non-banded rocks (basalt, granite) stay dark all the way through.
Hardness Agate is roughly a 7 on the Mohs scale — a steel knife blade or glass edge should not scratch it. If your fingernail or a knife easily scratches the surface, it isn't agate.
Shape & texture Because Lake Superior agates were carried by glaciers and tumbled by waves, they're usually found as smooth, rounded pebbles or cobbles rather than sharp, freshly broken chunks.
Where to look Cobble beaches, gravel bars, road cuts, and gravel pits throughout the Lake Superior watershed (Minnesota's North Shore, Wisconsin, Michigan's Upper Peninsula, and Ontario) — anywhere glacial till or lake-washed gravel is exposed.
